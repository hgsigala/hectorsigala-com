The plan

Four days, at family speed

One base for all three nights — the Begur peninsula — so nobody repacks a bag until Monday.

How to read these times

Every time below is already a with-kids time — shoes lost, snack negotiated, one more swim granted. Nothing on this trip is a tour or a ticket window, so treat the times as a rhythm, not a schedule. There are exactly three real clocks all weekend: the Aiguablava parking lot before 9:45 on Saturday, whatever dinner tables you booked, and Monday's ~2 pm pull-out for the 4 pm Barcelona arrival. Everything else can slide an hour — the coves aren't going anywhere, and the whole point of driving instead of flying is that nobody's holding a boarding pass.

The last stretch is the point: from Roses the road climbs over the bare Cap de Creus massif in a run of hairpins — olive terraces, slate, sea on both horizons. Whoever isn't driving gets the camera.

    Swimsuits under clothes this morning. Walk 20 minutes over the hill to Portlligat, the fishing hamlet where Dalí lived for 50 years — the white house with the giant eggs on the roof sits right on a calm, boat-dotted cove that's made for an unhurried swim. That's today's beach: one cove, no schedule, with a surrealist's house as the backdrop. (Backup if legs are short: Es Llané, the little beach directly below the Riba in town.) Only book the timed house-interior visit if you're sure the crew is up for it — it's the one thing in Cadaqués that would put you on a clock. Stroller: yes for this one — paved lane the whole way.

Anytime from 4 to 7 works — the later, the more copper the light. Parking: lot below the lighthouse, 5 minutes up the road on foot. Stroller: no — it's all rock; hand-holding terrain for the youngest.

    100 minutes back; the hairpins are gentler downhill. No target bedtime — tomorrow's only fixture is breakfast.

MI 294 / KM 473 · BASE CAMP · 3 NIGHTS

Begur

A hill town with a castle on top and eight coves at its feet
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why locals love it. Begur is the town Catalans keep for themselves while tourists pile into Lloret. It sits a kilometer back from the sea on a hill, so it kept its life: a real plaça with card games and vermut, "Indiano" mansions built by returners from Cuba, and lanes that smell of jasmine at night. Every road out of town falls to a different cove.

The evening ritual. Walk up to the castle ruin (15 min) for the 360° view — Pyrenees to one side, the whole Costa Brava to the other — then descend into the old town for dinner. That loop, repeated three nights, is the spine of the weekend.

Time needed. It's home base — you'll absorb it in evening doses, which is exactly how it's best taken.

MI 297 / KM 478 · A NEARBY COVE

Sa Tuna

A pebble cove, twenty white houses, and water like bottle glass
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why locals love it. Sa Tuna is what the Costa Brava looked like before anyone invented the resort: a fishermen's hamlet wedged into a crack in the cliffs, houses whitewashed by law, no hotels but one, no shops, no noise except halyards. Josep Pla wrote about it; nothing much has changed since.

The swim. Smooth pebbles, so the water is absurdly clear — bring water shoes for small feet. The rocky points on either side are the best easy snorkeling of the trip — Ivan and Gael will disappear along them, while the middle of the cove stays Mayu-and-Illa calm. Morning light is the good light and the calm water.

The walk. A 20-minute camí de ronda path rounds the cliff to the next cove, Aiguafreda — do it before lunch while the kids build pebble towers.

Time needed. Half a day — an easy morning swap from Aiguablava if you want a change of cove, and the trip's best snorkeling.

MI 303 / KM 488 · SATURDAY · YOUR BEACH DAY

Aiguablava

The postcard the whole coast is judged against
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why locals love it. The name means "blue water" and undersells it. A wide, shallow wedge of coarse golden sand facing exactly the right way, pines to the tide line, and the village of Fornells hanging over the far side of the bay. If the family has "already experienced Mediterranean beaches," this is the one that re-runs the experiment with better inputs.

For the kids. The sandy bottom shelves gently for a long way — the most relaxed supervising of the trip. Kayak and SUP rentals on the sand in summer.

The sunset move. No sunset over the water on this coast — instead, drinks on the Parador terrace on the cliff above while the bay goes pink below. Kids get the pool-deck view; adults get five minutes of silence.

Time needed. All day — this is your Saturday beach. Picnic on the sand whenever the kids are hungry; nobody's on a clock until you head up for a relaxed family dinner in Begur.

MI 309 / KM 497 · FLEX STOP · ANY EVENING

Calella de Palafrugell

White arcades, beached fishing boats, and the coast's prettiest walking path
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why locals love it. Ten minutes from Begur, Calella is the village the havaneres — Catalan sailors' songs — come home to. Boats are still winched up onto the sand under the whitewashed arcades of Port Bo, and the town has fiercely resisted every tall building ever proposed. It's the liveliest of the coves: shops, terraces, families promenading at 8 pm.

The walk. The camí de ronda from Port Bo south to the Golfet tunnel, or north 25 minutes to Llafranc's marina — flat enough for everyone, ocean the whole way. Go at 7 pm when the light goes honey-colored and dinner is the destination.

Fits where? Slot it as a second-evening outing, or swap it into Saturday if the Aiguablava lot defeats you.

Time needed. An evening — walk, swim off Canadell, dinner.

MI 368 / KM 593 · SUNDAY

Cadaqués

The white town at the end of the mountain road — Dalí's, and now yours
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why locals love it. Cadaqués is hard to get to — one twisting road over the Cap de Creus massif — and that road is why it's still perfect. Too far for day-tripping crowds from Barcelona, it stayed a painters' town: white houses, slate streets polished by 500 years of feet, blue shutters by decree. Dalí summered here as a boy and settled next door in Portlligat for fifty years; Picasso, Miró, Duchamp and García Lorca all came through.

The drive in. From Roses the road hairpins up through olive terraces and bare rock with the sea appearing on both sides — the single most scenic 30 minutes of driving on the trip. Queasy kids in front.

The walking route. Land on the Riba waterfront → coffee → climb the lanes to Santa Maria church (the view back over the bay is the town's best) → over the hill 20 minutes to Portlligat and the egg-topped Dalí house → loop back for lunch. All of it unhurried: two and a half hours.

Time needed. The full Sunday, paired with Cap de Creus.

MI 373 / KM 601 · SUNDAY EVENING

Cap de Creus

Mainland Spain's easternmost rock — a lighthouse, wind-carved cliffs, the end of the map
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why it's worth the extra 20 minutes. Past Cadaqués the road narrows to a single lane through a landscape Dalí painted over and over because he didn't have to invent anything: slate folded into waves, rocks eroded into animals, no trees, two horizons of sea. At the tip stands the 1853 lighthouse and below it, cliffs dropping into water so clear the boats look parked on air.

What you do. Park below the lighthouse, then wander — smooth rock paths lead to coves and outcrops in every direction, and the kids will treat it as the planet's best climbing gym (watch edges; it's a natural park, not a playground). The bar-restaurant in the lighthouse compound does drinks with the best table in Spain.

The light. Late afternoon turns the whole cape copper, and it's the first place in Spain the sun rises — sunset behind the hills is still spectacular, but golden hour is the show. Leave by 7 for the early Cadaqués dinner plan.

Time needed. Two hours, plus the drive both ways.

MI 416 / KM 670 · MONDAY MORNING

Pals

A thousand-year-old hill town, restored stone by golden stone
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

Why locals love it. Pals was a half-ruined medieval village until the 1950s, when one determined doctor spent decades restoring it. The result is the Empordà's most complete Gothic quarter: ramp-smooth stone lanes, pointed arches, flower pots on every sill, and the round Torre de les Hores keeping time over it all. Yes, other people visit it — on a Monday morning before the buses, it's yours.

The walking route. Enter at the bottom of Carrer Major → spiral up past the church of Sant Pere → Torre de les Hores → finish at the Mirador Josep Pla, where the view runs over rice paddies to the Medes islands. Forty-five slow minutes; the town rewards zero planning.

The detail for the kids. Pals grows rice — the arroz on every menu here was in a paddy you can see from the mirador. Order it at Vicus and close the loop.

Time needed. 90 minutes plus lunch — a perfect final morning.

Barcelona

Roll in rested, fed, and full of coves — at 4 pm as promised
⌖ Open in Google Maps
Photo: Wikimedia Commons

The C-32 delivers you into the city with the sea still on your left, at 4 pm on the dot. Sequence matters with seven people: Airbnb first, everyone and everything out of the car, then an hour or two of doing absolutely nothing. Only then does one adult run the car to the rental office — Sants station desks keep evening hours, and the metro or a ten-minute taxi brings you home. Gelato on the walk back; Gaudí tomorrow.

Arriving by road on a Monday afternoon beats arriving at the airport in every measurable way: no baggage claim, no taxi line for seven, and everyone already napped in the car.

Decision made — home base for all three nights is El Colomar, a whole villa with a private pool on the hill above Begur. The other places below aren't beds; two of them are terraces already on your itinerary, worth knowing by name.

Booked · your base

El Colomar · Son Rich ⌖

Whole villa · sleeps 8 · private pool

A hilltop villa in Son Rich, 1.5 km from Begur old town, with panoramic sea views and a private pool in the garden. Three double suites plus a single-and-trundle room, four bathrooms — room for all seven. Full kitchen and dishwasher for the villa breakfasts, plus a barbecue, parking and a laundry. A few minutes down to the old-town dinners and Fitzroy, and central to every cove — Sa Riera, Sa Tuna, Aiguablava, Aiguafreda.

📍 Carrer Josep Maria Pi 6, Begur 17253 ⌖

Backup · if plans change

Tamariu or Llafranc apartment/house

Beachfront-village alternative

If the villa ever fell through: quieter Tamariu or polished Llafranc, walk-to-beach, Calella one cove over for the evening promenade. Trade-off: 15 min instead of 8 to Sa Tuna / Aiguablava mornings.

On your itinerary anyway

Parador de Aiguablava ⌖

Sunset drinks

The modernist state hotel alone on the cliff above Aiguablava — you're using its terrace Saturday at 7:30 pm. It's also a room you could book, if you ever want the pure-view version of this trip.

On your itinerary anyway

Hostal Sa Tuna & Hotel Aigua Blava ⌖

Lunch · a view

Hotel Aigua Blava sits on Fornells point right above the Aiguablava bay — a garden-view lunch or drink if the adults want one; Hostal Sa Tuna is the little restaurant down at Sa Tuna cove. Both are also five-room-scale stays worth remembering for a couples' return trip.

The kids clause — every lunch & dinner

Spain has a universal kids' menu that isn't printed anywhere: tortilla francesa (a plain French omelette — their scrambled eggs), grilled chicken with fries, and plain pasta. Every kitchen on this itinerary, from beach hostal to Michelin-listed Vicus, will make these on request — the move is to order the kids' food with the drinks, before the adult order, so it lands first and buys the table an hour. When booking La Pizzeta or Vicus, mention four kids; nobody on this coast blinks at a family table.

All menu links point at each restaurant's own site (or its listing page where no site exists — Fitzroy, Casa Anita); URLs verified live July 13. Wine everywhere: ask for Empordà DO, the region's own. Order L'Escala anchovies wherever you see them. Book Vicus ahead — La Pizzeta's already done; everything else is walk-in.
